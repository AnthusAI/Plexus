# A Self-Aligning Meta-Cognition Loop for Scorecard Optimization

**Anthus AI Solutions Research · April 2026**

Two questions keep surfacing for teams running production classifiers at scale. First: when a classifier scorecard needs to be brought into alignment with its subject-matter experts, how quickly can we get there — and can we do that without pulling a senior prompt engineer off other work for a week? Second: as scores proliferate across a scorecard, and scorecards proliferate across an account, who actually holds the theory of why each score behaves the way it does? There aren't enough humans to go around, and human-written configurations decay as the underlying content stream shifts.

In short: alignment, for everyday classification work, has a labor problem and an institutional-memory problem. These aren't the exotic superintelligence framings of the frontier, but the everyday version most organizations will encounter first.

In a new Anthus research effort built on top of the open-source Plexus platform, we pursue both problems together. We ask whether a scientific process — hypothesis, implementation, test — can be delegated to an AI agent that improves a score's configuration over many cycles, and whether the *meta*-process — the question of which hypothesis strategies work best — can itself be learned empirically by running the optimizer many times and letting a second layer of analysis compare the runs.

We call the result a **self-aligning meta-cognition loop**. The inner loop aligns a score to human feedback. The outer loop aligns the optimizer's own methodology to what has actually moved metrics across previous runs. Both loops are executed by LLM-driven agents, orchestrated by a domain-specific runtime we call Tactus. The combination lets a single production score improve overnight, and — more interestingly — lets the optimization strategy itself improve from one score to the next.

## Our setup

The inner loop is a four-phase cycle that iterates until a budget is exhausted or accuracy plateaus. In the **planning** phase, a planner agent receives a rich context bundle: the current YAML configuration of the score, the written guidelines, a confusion matrix split by segment, a root-cause analysis of recent misclassifications, and — critically — a *prior-run prescription* summarizing what has and hasn't worked on earlier cycles. It emits between one and five concrete hypotheses, each tagged as an incremental rule tweak, a bolder overhaul, or a structural change to the score's graph. In the **implementation** phase, an editor agent edits the YAML through a ReAct tool loop, pushes a new version, and runs a five-item smoke test to catch crashes before spending evaluation budget. In the **synthesis** phase, every surviving candidate is evaluated against two independent datasets: a frozen regression set that protects against silent backsliding, and a recent-feedback set that measures genuine alignment with SMEs. In the **review** phase, strict acceptance rules are applied: a candidate must improve the feedback metric by at least half a percentage point without regressing the accuracy metric by more than two, and must not lose more than five points against the previous champion. Accepted versions become the new baseline; the cycle repeats.

The outer loop is newer, and it is where the meta-cognition lives. We run the inner loop many times — across different scores, different scorecards, different customer domains — and log every hypothesis, every diff, every metric delta, and every acceptance decision into a structured corpus. A second agent, the escalation advisor, reads this corpus and answers a different question: *given what we've seen across runs, which planning strategies, hypothesis categories, and escalation modes actually produce accepted changes, and under what conditions?* Its output reshapes the prompts and slot allocations of future planner agents. The optimizer, in other words, supervises itself.

## Results

On a representative set of production scorecards drawn from customer engagements covering call-center compliance, editorial quality review, and clinical intake triage, the inner loop closes the gap between baseline AC1 and the SME ceiling by **[X]** on average, where 1.0 represents fully recovering the observable alignment headroom and 0.0 represents no improvement. A human prompt engineer working the same scores for a comparable wall-clock budget closes **[Y]** of the same gap. The optimizer runs for roughly **[N] hours** of agent time per score at an average inference cost of about **[$Z]**, against an estimated **[$W]** of engineer time at fully loaded rates.

Two results deserve closer attention. The first is that the dual-dataset evaluation caught regressions that a single metric would have missed: in **[P%]** of accepted candidates, feedback-AC1 improved while accuracy-AC1 dropped enough that a naive acceptance rule would have shipped a worse score. The second is that item-recurrence tracking — the practice of labeling each misclassified item as OSCILLATING, PERSISTENT, FLIP_FLOP, or LATE_EMERGING across cycles — identified a cohort of items that no configuration change ever resolved. These items map almost perfectly to cases where SMEs themselves disagreed on the gold label, confirming empirically that the optimizer's plateau coincides with the real contradiction ceiling rather than an agent limitation.

The outer loop is where our view of the system actually changed. After running the inner loop across **[M]** distinct scores, the escalation advisor surfaced several patterns we had not anticipated. Bold-overhaul hypotheses were accepted at a lower rate than incremental ones, but when a bold overhaul was accepted, its subsequent cycles were measurably more productive than cycles following an incremental accept. Structural graph changes rarely worked in the first few cycles of a run, but became the dominant accepted category in later cycles — suggesting that the planner should suppress them early and favor them late. The advisor began injecting this schedule into prior-run prescriptions automatically. Over subsequent runs, average per-run cost and wall-clock time both dropped, with no loss of final accuracy.

We were not expecting the outer loop to find scheduling effects; we expected it to find prompt-level lessons. That we found structural timing effects instead is, for us, the clearest evidence that the meta-cognition layer is doing work a human reviewer would have missed.

## Implications

**Keeping pace.** The most immediate implication is operational. An alignment loop that costs single-digit dollars per score per night means scorecards can be continuously re-aligned as feedback accumulates, rather than re-aligned in quarterly engineering sprints. This changes the economics of running a large scorecard library: the marginal cost of maintaining a score approaches the marginal cost of collecting its feedback, instead of being dominated by engineer availability. For the teams we work with, this is the difference between maintaining tens of scores and maintaining hundreds.

**Taste and diversity.** One critique of automated prompt optimization is that it lacks *research taste* — the intuition a practitioner develops about which directions are promising. Our meta-cognition results suggest a practical substitute: let many optimizers run many hypotheses, and let an empirical advisor decide which categories of hypothesis are worth the budget. Individual planner agents have no taste. The escalation advisor, operating over a corpus of accepted and rejected diffs, develops something close to it. It is not a single agent's taste; it is the statistical shadow of many agents' attempts, and it becomes the prior for all future attempts. This is the inversion we find most interesting: taste emerges from volume rather than compensating for its absence.

**Alien science.** Some of the accepted configurations the optimizer produces are not how a human prompt engineer would write them. They include redundant conditions, oddly ordered enumerations, and negatively-phrased rules where a positive phrasing would read more naturally. In evaluation, these configurations beat the hand-written ones. We have resisted the urge to refactor them for readability, because the lesson of the meta-cognition layer is that our aesthetic preferences about prompt style are not always correlated with AC1. A YAML that looks wrong to a senior engineer but produces a consistent alignment gain across a regression set is, by the definition we care about, correct. This is uncomfortable, and we think it should be.

**Preventing hacks.** A loop that optimizes a score against its own regression set will, given enough cycles, find the regression set. We have seen early symptoms of this: configurations that produce slightly suspicious improvements on the regression dataset while barely moving feedback alignment. The acceptance rules are designed to make this hard — feedback-AC1 gates acceptance, a fresh held-out subset of feedback is rotated in each run, and any accepted change must justify itself against both datasets — but no such rule is airtight. As with any optimizer, the objective is a proxy, and the proxy is exploitable. We treat every accepted champion as a candidate for human spot-review before it enters production, and the system supports this workflow natively. Automation moves the human role upstream, toward evaluating changes rather than generating them, but it does not remove the human.

---

The shift we want to mark is a shift in where the intelligence of the system lives. Classical prompt engineering puts it in the engineer. Single-pass automated prompt optimization puts it in a prompt-optimizer agent. The architecture described here puts a portion of it in a corpus — a growing record of what has and hasn't worked across scores, scorecards, and domains — and gives later runs the benefit of earlier ones without human curation in the middle. The optimizer is not simply trying things; it is trying things in the light of what previous optimizers have learned, and the rules it follows to decide what to try next are themselves subject to revision.

We think this is the right shape for alignment work on the scales most organizations will actually face: not superhuman oversight, but thousands of classifiers that need to stay in agreement with thousands of human reviewers over time, at a cost that makes continuous alignment economically rational.

One direction we are pursuing next is cross-customer transfer: whether the meta-cognition corpus built from one customer's scorecards generalizes to a new customer's first scorecard, and how strongly the advisor's prescriptions should be weighted before that customer has accumulated its own history. Early indications are that structural scheduling effects transfer more readily than domain-specific rule phrasings, as expected.

A second direction is letting the outer loop modify more of the inner loop than the planner prompts. The escalation advisor currently rewrites planning prescriptions and adjusts hypothesis-slot allocations. It does not yet modify the editor agent's tool set, the acceptance thresholds, or the structure of the confusion-matrix context. Each of these is a control surface the meta-cognition layer could learn over, if we give it the interface and enough runs to stabilize.

We are releasing the optimizer procedure, the Tactus runtime, and the MCP tooling that drives it as part of the open-source Plexus distribution. A walk-through of a single end-to-end run, with the full hypothesis log and acceptance trace, is available in the project's documentation, and the escalation-advisor corpus format is specified so that teams running their own Plexus deployments can contribute to — and benefit from — the same meta-cognition layer.
